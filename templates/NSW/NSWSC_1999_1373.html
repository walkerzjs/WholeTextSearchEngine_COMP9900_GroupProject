Regina v T R [1999] NSWSC 1373 (28 July 1999)

REGINA v. T.R

60606/95

Friday, 1 November 1996

IN THE COURT OF CRIMINAL APPEAL

HUNT CJ AT CL, SMART, IRELAND JJ

CATCHWORDS: SUPPLY OF LARGE COMMERCIAL QUANTITY OF COCAINE-PLEA OF GUILTY-ASSISTANCE GIVEN BY APPLICANT TO AUTHORITIES - SPECIAL CIRCUMSTANCES OF APPLICANT

EX TEMPORE/RESERVED: Reserved

ALLOWED/DISMISSED: Allowed.

HEADNOTE

The applicant applied for an extension of time and leave to appeal against the severity of a sentence of 6 years, comprising a minimum term of 3 years 9 months and an additional term of 2 years 3 months, for the offence of supplying not less than a large commercial quantity of cocaine.

Held:

1. Having regard to the valuable assistance provided as to the subject offence and those involved, the assistance provided in relation to other matters of much importance, the full significance and use of that latter assistance now being revealed and the statement made to the applicant as to how he would be treated and confined, this is a very rare case where a greater discount should be given than that allowed by the judge.

2. This case also involved special circumstances. These include the fact that this is the applicant's first custodial sentence, that he is a young man and will need considerable support, supervision and protection on his release, that he will have to be alert to maintain his own security and that of his family and that this is likely to place restrictions on his movements after release.

Orders:

1. Extend the time within which the applicant may apply for leave to appeal against sentence.

2. Leave to appeal granted.

3. Appeal allowed.

4. In lieu of the sentence imposed, the applicant is sentenced to five years imprisonment comprising a minimum term of 2 years 8 months starting on 29 July 1994 and ending on 28 March 1997 when the applicant will be eligible for release on parole and an additional term of 2 years 4 months commencing on 29 March 1997 and ending on 28 July 1999.

5. Order that the Supplementary Reasons be placed in a sealed envelope within the court file not to be opened save with the leave of a judge of the Court of Criminal Appeal and that the envelope be endorsed accordingly. A copy of the Supplementary Reasons is to be made available to the sentencing judge and to each of the Director of Public Prosecutions for perusal by him and his nominated officers and the solicitor for the applicant for perusal by the applicant's legal advisers and the applicant. They are not to be otherwise published or distributed except with the leave of a judge of the Court of Criminal Appeal.

JUDGMENT

HUNT CJ at CL: I agree with the orders proposed by Smart J, for the reasons which he has given.

SMART J: TR has applied for an extension of time and leave to appeal against the severity of a sentence imposed by the District Court following his plea of guilty to a charge of between 9 February and 29 July 1994 supplying not less than a large commercial quantity of cocaine and the Court taking into account three matters relating to the possession of a firearm, ammunition and an ampoule of steroid. TR received a total sentence of 6 years comprising a minimum term of 3 years 9 months and an additional term of 2 years 3 months. The maximum penalty is life imprisonment.

At the sentence hearing the estimated quantity of 2000 grams was acknowledged not to be precise but the applicant's approach was that even assuming the quantity should be somewhat less it would nonetheless come within the definition of a large commercial quantity.

The applicant had been under telephone surveillance from February 1994 until his arrest on 29 July 1994. Gradually the picture emerged. The judge has described in some detail the applicant's method of operation. In summary, the applicant purchased supplies of cocaine and then caused small quantities to be sold to street level drug users. He operated through two low cost hotels. The customer attended at the reception desk and indicated his requirements. After payment of the nominated amount the customer was directed to a part of the premises where the quantity of the drug was handed over together with a clean syringe, swabs, plastic spoons and an ampoule of saline solution. If desired, a room could be obtained in which to administer the drug. This cost a further $5.00. A lot of sales were made.

Cash had been transferred by the applicant or his family to another country. Substantial assets have been seized from the prisoner and his family who have co-operated with the authorities. Indeed, the prisoner's co-operation has resulted in $380,000 being recovered and forfeited to the State. A solicitor with the Crime Commission described the level of co-operation as being of the highest order.

The applicant was aged about 21 at the time of the offences. He was a principal if not the principal in a highly organised and quite sophisticated drug selling operation. He controlled and directed it. There was a strong case against the applicant but his plea of guilty saved considerable court and police time and much public expense. As a result of his assistance seven or eight other persons were arrested and charged in connection with the offence of supplying a large commercial quantity of cocaine. The applicant undertook to give evidence in all proceedings against his co-accused.

The judge recorded that the applicant had offered other assistance of an unusually high level to the authorities. The judge found that this and the applicant's admissions at an early stage demonstrated substantial contrition on his part. The judge was satisfied that, as a result of the assistance given and to be given, the applicant's life was in danger and members of his family were also at risk. The threats made to his safety had been taken very seriously as had the threats to his family.

The subjective features found by the judge included that the applicant, although born in Australia, spent four years of his boyhood in a war torn country. The family were driven out by the bombing and eventually returned to Australia where they lived in uncomfortable conditions for some time. He left school at the age of 16. He was intelligent and had marked natural skills. The judge thought that given his contrition and that his drug activities had been stopped at an early age there were significant prospects of rehabilitation. Further, other criminals are unlikely to want to work with him. The applicant's prior record comprised two minor offences unrelated to drugs. They were dealt with by a small fine and a recognizance respectively.

The judge took into account that the applicant would serve his sentence in considerable isolation and that his family, for security reasons may have to live at some distance from him thereby reducing the frequency of family contact.

Correctly, much emphasis was placed on the gravity of the offence and the harmful consequences to others. The judge thought that an appropriate total sentence was eleven years. That sentence, was reduced firstly by twelve months for the substantial co-operation in the recovery of the crime proceeds and secondly by a further four years for the substantial assistance provided and to be provided to the authorities. The remaining six years were apportioned as earlier mentioned.

There was no challenge to the starting point of eleven years by the applicant or the Crown and it was one which was well within the sound exercise of the court's discretion. On the material before the judge the sentence imposed was not open to attack.

For compelling security reasons the authorities could not in their advice to the sentencing court indicate fully the significance of the assistance which the applicant had given in areas other than that related to the particular offence. The authorities considered that it was not prudent even to indicate that an operation of considerable public importance was on foot given the applicant's identity. Neither party could be told that an adjournment should be sought. This necessarily meant that the judge was not told. Subsequent to the hearing before the judge and after the operation became a matter of public knowledge the full significance became apparent. The assistance was of much value and helped to expose a wide range of criminal conduct and the many involved. It also provided some solid and truthful evidence. The assistance has continued. It suffices to say of the assistance that its extent and importance rank it amongst the most valuable assistance provided to the authorities. In furtherance of the protection of the applicant and his family and the proceedings in train and to be instituted and the further investigations by the authorities I have included the details in supplementary reasons to be given to the parties and the sentencing judge but otherwise to be placed in a sealed envelope not to be opened or otherwise published except with the leave of the court. There is one further matter. Initially, a broad statement was made to the applicant as to how he would be treated and confined. It was so broad that it was understood in a sense different from that which may have been intended. That statement has not been able to be met according to its terms as understood. His present incarceration is somewhat more severe.

Having regard to the valuable assistance provided as to the subject offence and those involved, the assistance provided in relation to other matters of much importance, the full significance and use of that latter assistance now being revealed, and the statement mentioned, this is that very rare case where a greater discount should be given than that allowed by the judge. The term "very rare" is used advisedly.

The appropriate starting point remains a total sentence of 11 years. The reduction of one year allowed by the judge for the assistance to recover the crime proceeds is sound. The discount for the matters referred to in the preceding paragraph should be five years, resulting in a total sentence of five years. There are special circumstances. This is the applicant's first custodial sentence. He is a young man and he will need considerable support, supervision and protection on his release. He will have to re-train. With his obvious intelligence that may involve considerable study to obtain qualifications that will enable him to undertake satisfying work. He will have to be alert to maintain his own security and that of his family. That is likely to result in restrictions on his movements even after release. The sentence should comprise a minimum term of 2 years 8 months and an additional term of 2 years 4 months. I have taken into account the offences mentioned on Form 2 to which reference has previously been made.

I propose the following orders:

1. Extend the time within which the applicant may apply for leave to appeal against sentence.

2. Leave to appeal granted.

3. Appeal allowed.

4. In lieu of the sentence imposed, the applicant is sentenced to five years imprisonment comprising a minimum term of 2 years 8 months starting on 29 July 1994 and ending on 28 March 1997 when the applicant will be eligible for release on parole and an additional term of 2 years 4 months commencing on 29 March 1997 and ending on 28 July 1999.

5. Order that the Supplementary Reasons be placed in a sealed envelope within the court file not to be opened save with the leave of a judge of the Court of Criminal Appeal and that the envelope be endorsed accordingly. A copy of the Supplementary Reasons is to be made available to the sentencing judge and to each of the Director of Public Prosecutions for perusal by him and his nominated officers and the solicitor for the applicant for perusal by the applicant's legal advisers and the applicant. They are not to be otherwise published or distributed except with the leave of a judge of the Court of Criminal Appeal.

IRELAND J: I agree with the orders proposed by Smart J and with the reasons therefor.
