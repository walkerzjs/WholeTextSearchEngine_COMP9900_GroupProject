Regina v David Tom Hall [1999] NSWSC 1372 (26 July 1999)

REGINA v David Tom HALL

60014/96

Thursday 17 April 1997

COURT OF CRIMINAL APPEAL

Simpson J, Hidden J

CATCHWORDS: CRIMINAL LAW - Sentence - applicant's physical health - little or no evidence before sentencing judge - further evidence received on appeal - exceptional circumstances, no question of principle - appeal allowed.

EX TEMPORE/RESERVED: Reserved

ALLOWED/DISMISSED: Allowed

JUDGMENT

SIMPSON J: I agree with the orders of Justice Hidden and his reasons therefor.

HIDDEN J:

This is an application for leave to appeal against sentences passed upon the applicant after he pleaded guilty before Nield DCJ at Liverpool District Court to a variety of charges. The charges and sentences were as follows:

1. Culpable driving: fixed term of 6 months imprisonment for 27 July 1995 (licence disqualified for 3 years).

2. Break enter and steal: fixed term of 3 years penal servitude from 27 July 1995.

3. Break enter and steal: penal servitude for 5 years, comprising a minimum term of 3 years from 27 July 1995 and an additional term of 2 years.

4. Attempt break and enter with intent to steal: fixed term of 3 years penal servitude from 27 July 1995.

5. Possess housebreaking implements: fixed term of 1 year from 27 July 1995.

6. Assault police officer in the execution of his duty: fixed term of 2 years imprisonment from 27 July 1995.

An existing periodic detention order was cancelled and his Honour directed that the balance of that term be served concurrently with the sentences which he had imposed. The effective overall sentence then was a minimum term of 3 years from 27 July 1995, and an additional term of 2 years.

Facts.

The facts of the various offences can be briefly stated. The charge of culpable driving arose from a head-on collision between a vehicle driven by the applicant and another vehicle in the Wellington area on 21 December 1992. It seems that the applicant fell asleep at the wheel, having earlier been warned by a police officer that it was not safe to drive because he was tired. As a result his defacto wife suffered serious injuries. He himself was injured, a matter to which it will be necessary to return.

On 14 July 1995, he broke into adjoining home units at Casula and stole property, this activity giving rise to the two charges of break enter and steal. On 27 July 1995, he attempted to break into a home at Bonnyrigg with intent to steal, but was disturbed while doing so. As police sought to apprehend him, he brandished an axe and swung it towards one of the officers. He was disarmed and arrested and, upon being searched, was found to be in possession of a screwdriver. It was this incident which gave rise to the charges of attempt break and enter with intent, assault police and possess housebreaking implements (the axe and the screwdriver). His Honour expressed himself satisfied that the applicant carried the axe for the purpose of housebreaking, not for assault.

At the time of the culpable driving the applicant was on a recognisance granted in September 1992 in respect of a charge of entering enclosed lands with intent. At the time of the later offences he was serving periodic detention imposed in May 1995 on charges of assault occasioning actual bodily harm, break and enter and possess housebreaking implements. Further, after his arrest for the break and enter offences of 14 July 1995, he was released on bail and was subject to that bail undertaking at the time of the offences of 27 July.

Subjective Matters.

The applicant is now 34 years old. He has a criminal record which is quite sporadic in earlier years, containing entries in 1977, 1980 and 1983. Nothing further is recorded until September 1992, when he was convicted of the charge of enter enclosed land with intent, referred to above. In 1993 he was twice before courts on charges of break enter and steal, and in 1995 he was convicted of a minor matter to which I need not refer, and was dealt with for the matters previously mentioned for which he was sentenced to periodic detention for 12 months.

His relationship with his defacto wife was of long standing and there are four children of that union. He is of limited education and, over the years, has had various labouring jobs and some period of unemployment.

The applicant did not give evidence before his Honour, but he told a probation officer that he had used marijuana since his mid teens and had taken to heroin after the car accident. He told police that the 1995 offences were the result of his drug problem, which had left him unable to provide for his children, and his Honour appears to have accepted this. The applicant's brother, a drug and alcohol counsellor with the Aboriginal Medical Service at Redfern, gave evidence that he became addicted to morphine whilst in hospital after the car accident, and that after his discharge the applicant had "taken certain drugs to help him with his pain and the addiction from morphine". It is not entirely clear from the remarks on sentence what his Honour's assessment of this evidence was. As I read them, it seems that his Honour was prepared to accept that his treatment with morphine in hospital led to his heroin addiction, even though there was no evidence to confirm it.

The brother said that the applicant had more recently come to realise that his drug abuse was harming his family and himself and was motivated to address the problem. Before his Honour was a drug and alcohol report from the Department of Corrective Services to the same effect, and there were also a number of certificates attesting to his participation in educational courses whilst in custody. Those steps towards rehabilitation and his need for counselling in relation to his drug problem led his Honour to find special circumstances, so as to fix a longer than usual additional term.

The Application.

Before us the applicant was represented by Mr Segal of counsel, who did not appear for him in the District Court. The effective sentence is challenged only on the basis that it fails to give adequate weight to the applicant's physical injuries arising from the car accident, and their effect upon the conditions of his custody.

Apart from the brief reference to his physical injuries in the evidence of the applicant's brother, which I have mentioned, there was no evidence before his Honour on this matter. As I have said, the applicant gave no evidence, and there was no medical evidence. It seems that the injuries were referred to by the solicitor then appearing for the applicant, both in written and oral submissions, which led his Honour to observe in his remarks:

"It is the fact that, as a result of the motor vehicle collision on 21 December 1992, he suffered injuries and that those injuries have resulted in some permanent disabilities. I do not think, however, in the absence of medical evidence, that his disabilities are as severe as they have been painted. If they were I would have expected that medical evidence would have been produced."

It is impossible to challenge that finding on the material that was before his Honour. We have been supplied with a copy of the solicitor's written submissions which make laconic reference to the applicant's injuries, but we cannot know what was put in oral submissions as they have not been transcribed. However, before us, Mr Segal had relied upon an affidavit of the applicant sworn in December 1996, setting out his injuries and their effects and the applicant expanded that material somewhat in oral evidence. Again, no medical evidence was tendered.

The applicant's affidavit, in addition to setting out his injuries, recounts an incident when he suffered violence in prison on January 1996, and asserts that he is susceptible to serious injury if assaulted. In his oral evidence he spoke of another incident of violence in December 1996, which led to his requiring surgical treatment. Mr Segal submitted that this evidence was admissible as fresh evidence in accordance with the principles enunciated by King CJ in R v Smith (1987) 44 SASR 587 at 588. In a frequently cited passage, the Chief Justice held that it was permissible for an appellate court to have regard to events occurring after sentence for the purpose of demonstrating the true significance of a state of affairs which existed at the time of sentence and, in particular, so as to explain "the full extent and implications" of an illness which the prisoner suffered at the time of sentence. Mr Segal frankly explained that the absence of any medical evidence before us was the result of the funding constraints upon the regional Aboriginal Legal Aid organisation which was instructing him.

No doubt in recognition of the exceptional nature of the case, the Crown prosecutor, fairly in my view, did not object to our receiving the affidavit and the oral evidence. Equally, our reception of the evidence is exceptional and should in no way be seen as a precedent. The principle in Smith is not a licence for legal practitioners to make passing reference in proceedings at first instance to a matter about which evidence could have been led, and then ventilate the matter more fully on appeal. In the context of a prisoner's illness, King CJ's remarks were directed to cases where there was evidence of the illness before the sentencing court but the full significance of the condition was not understood until after sentence was passed, or where events after sentence established that the prisoner suffered an illness at the time of sentence of which he was not then aware: cf Bailey (1988) 36 A Crim R 458. The principles which govern the admission of fresh evidence in applications for leave to appeal against sentence are no different from those applicable to appeals against conviction: R v Lanham [1970] 2 NSWR 217.

In his affidavit the applicant deposes that, as a result of the car accident, he suffered fractures to his cheekbones, nose, jaw, hip and pelvis, punctured lungs and injuries to his right knee and foot. The fractures to his cheekbones required reconstructive surgery and the insertion of plates into his cheeks. As a result of the injuries to his lower limbs he walks with a limp, is susceptible to arthritis and is in constant pain. He deposes also that he is liable to serious injury if assaulted and, in the nature of things, the risk of violence in prison is high.

In his oral evidence he said that, as a result of the incident in December 1996 referred to above, the plates in his cheeks were loosened and had to be removed. He had not sought protection as he believed that he was at risk of assault by protected prisoners as much as by others. He acknowledged that he had discussed the possibility of protection only with other prisoners and not with prison staff.

In cross examination, the Crown prosecutor did not challenge the applicant's account of his injuries or of the two violent incidents. Given that he had only late notice of the affidavit and there was no medical evidence, it is difficult to see how he could have done so. Nevertheless, there is no reason why the applicant's evidence, even though uncorroborated, should not be accepted.

On the material before him it could not be said that Nield DCJ dealt with the applicant other than humanely. As the Crown prosecutor properly reminded us, the offences were serious and were committed whilst the applicant was subject to one form of conditional liberty or another. Nevertheless, because of the applicant's evidence before us about his injuries and the additional risk to which he is exposed whilst he is in custody, and for no other reason, I consider that this Court should intervene. The authorities on the relevance to sentence of a prisoner's state of health were reviewed by this Court in Reg v L (unreported, 17 June 1996) at pp 6-8, and it is unnecessary to repeat what is there set out.

In the event of resentence, we also received in evidence further certificates attesting to the applicant's participation in the prison's drug and alcohol programme and in educational courses. Taking all these matters into account, I have determined that the appropriate overall sentence is one of penal servitude for 4 years, comprising a minimum term of 2 years and an additional term of 2 years. To that end I would propose the following orders:

1. Leave is granted and the appeal allowed.

2. On the charge of break enter and steal from the premises of Mr Reddich on 14 July 1995, the sentence is quashed and the applicant is sentenced to a fixed term of 2 years penal servitude from 27 July 1995.

3. On the charge of breaking entering and stealing from the premises of Miss Watson on 14 July 1995, the sentence is quashed and the applicant is sentenced to penal servitude for 4 years, comprising a minimum term of 2 years from 27 July 1995 and expiring on 26 July 1997, and an additional term of 2 years commencing on 27 July 1997 and expiring on 26 July 1999.

4. On the charge of attempted break and enter with intent to steal, the sentence is quashed and the applicant is sentenced to a fixed term of 2 years penal servitude to date from 27 July 1995.

5. The other sentences passed by Nield DCJ are confirmed.
