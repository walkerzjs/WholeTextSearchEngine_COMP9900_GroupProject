Regina v George Lattouf [1999] NSWSC 1382 (21 August 1999)

REGINA v George LATTOUF

60433/96

THURSDAY 12 DECEMBER 1996

IN THE COURT OF CRIMINAL APPEAL

MAHONEY ACJ, SULLY J, ADAMS AJ

CATCHWORDS: CRIMINAL LAW - Appeal by Crown against sentence - Seriousness of offence - Discretion of sentencing judge

RESULT: Appeal dismissed

Criminal law - Respondent pleaded guilty to armed robbery and sentenced to periodic detention - Appeal by Crown against sentence pursuant to s 5D of the Criminal Appeal Act 1912 - Sentencing principles - Seriousness of offence - Public perception - Discretion of sentencing judge - Whether appealable error in sentencing by trial judge -

Held: appeal dismissed.

(i) Taking into account the discretion of the sentencing judge, the principles governing sentencing appeals by the Crown, and the circumstances of the case, the Court is not satisfied that the sentence in the present case was wrong.

(ii) Observations on the seriousness of the crime of armed robbery usually requiring the imposition of a full-time custodial sentence, except in exceptional circumstances.

Kable v Director of Public Prosecutions (1995) 36 NSWLR 374; R v Rushby (1977) 1 NSWLR 594; R v Dodd (1991) 57 ACrimR 349; R v Readman (1990) 47 ACrimR 181; R v Roberts (1994) 73 ACrimR 306; referred to.

ORDER

Appeal dismissed.

JUDGMENT

MAHONEY ACJ: On 3 June 1995 Mr George Lattouf was concerned with two other men in an armed robbery. They were arrested. Mr Lattouf was charged with an offence under s 97(2) of the Crimes Act 1900: that on 3 June 1995 at Lakemba when armed with a dangerous weapon namely a revolver, he did rob the persons there named of $2,600 in cash and a quantity of electrical equipment. The maximum penalty for the offence is penal servitude for twenty-five years.

Mr Lattouf and the other participants in the robbery pleaded guilty. He was remanded in custody for some two months. On 27 June 1996 he was sentenced to periodic detention for a period of three years to commence on 5 July 1996.

One of the participants in the offence of armed robbery, Mr Pogorilic, was sentenced to a minimum term of penal servitude for four years to date from 4 June 1995 with an additional term of two years; Mr Attie, the other participant, was sentenced to a minimum term of penal servitude for four years to date from 22 August 1995 and, in respect of another offence, to a minimum term of penal servitude for twelve months to commence from 21 August 1999. As the sentencing judge said, there was an effective sentence of five years for Mr Attie and four years for Mr Pogorilic.

The Director of Public Prosecutions has appealed to this Court pursuant to s 5D of the Criminal Appeal Act 1912 against the sentence imposed upon Mr Lattouf.

The facts and circumstances giving rise to the sentences were stated by the sentencing judge his Honour Judge Johnston as follows:

"The facts and circumstances giving rise to these series of charges can best be summarised as follows: At about 4.15 pm on Saturday, 3 June 1995, the prisoners went to the Scorpio Car Audio and Communications store at 969 Canterbury Road, Lakemba. There they parked the vehicle they were driving, an Ascot hire utility hired from the BP service station at Kingsgrove in a laneway at the rear of the store. The prisoners then entered the store via a back door. Once inside they began to talk to the store salesman in relation to the purchase of car audio equipment. After all customers had left the store, Pogorilic removed from his clothing a large silver revolver. He then threatened staff and made demands for money. A quantity of money totalling $2600 was taken from a desk drawer. After removing the money, Lattouf and Attie commenced to remove equipment from inside the store to their vehicle.

Whilst this was taking place, Pogorilic remained inside the premises, holding the staff at bay with a pistol. Whilst Lattouf was outside loading equipment into the vehicle, Pogorilic fired a single shot from the pistol narrowly missing one of the staff members. He then ran from the store and joined the others at the vehicle parked in the laneway. The three offenders then left the area in the hired utility.

Lattouf stated that prior to them removing the stereo equipment from the warehouse floor, he remained near the doorway to the premises to act as a lookout. He claimed to only have received three hundred dollars from his part of the money. He said the money was to be divided evenly but he was told by his co-offenders that the proceeds only totalled one thousand.

The shot that was fired by Pogorilic hit a mobile metal pamphlet display unit and deflected off in a different direction, coming to rest on the carpet approximately four metres away. The salesman at the time was taking cover behind the display unit. The projectile failed to penetrate the unit and no person was injured.

Inquiries were made in regard to the utility. These inquiries revealed that the utility was hired on 27 May 1995 in the name of Fadre Attie from a service station on Kingsgrove Road, Kingsgrove. On Sunday, 4 June 1995, Pogorilic was arrested whilst attempting to return the utility to the service station. He was taken to the Lakemba Police Station and interviewed by way of ERISP. During the interview he made admissions as to his involvement. He was searched and located inside his jacket pocket was a small plastic bag containing four small plastic bags each containing a white powdery substance which he admitted was 'speed'.

The prisoner refused to supply the names of his co-offenders or the present location of the firearm. The finding of those four packages became the subject of the Form Two.

The prisoner, Attie was picked from a selection of photographs shown by way of video to the salesman, Mr Merry. On 3 August 1995 a vehicle belonging to the prisoner was located and found to contain property stolen during the armed robbery offence.

Inquiries by the investigating detectives revealed that the prisoner, Attie was living with his girlfriend, Nikki Thomasini and another couple on a five acre lot known as Lot 5, Mersey Road, Bringelly. A perimeter was established by the State Protection Group police and the prisoner was called upon to come out of the house by negotiators. Initially the prisoner was spoken to by telephone. However, he broke off communications with negotiators and was seen to walk into the backyard of the premises looking for police. A short time later the prisoner emerged from the front of the house armed with a knife and threatening his girlfriend. At the time he was holding the knife close to her. He forced her into a car which was parked nearby and placed her in the driver's seat. The prisoner got into the back seat and held the knife to her neck. He then made her drive the car a short distance. However, she managed to escape from the car and it was at this time that the prisoner was arrested by the State Protection Group police.

The prisoner was subsequently interviewed at Liverpool Police Station where he took part in an interview which was recorded by way ERISP. During the interview the prisoner admitted his involvement in the armed robbery at Scorpio Car Audio on 3 June 1995. He also admitted that he had armed himself with a knife and forced his girlfriend from the house and into the car for the purpose of attempting to avoid arrest by police."

From the remarks of the learned judge upon the sentencing proceeding the following facts emerged. Mr Lattouf was a single man aged nineteen at the time of the offence. He was residing with his family at Campsie. Apart from one minor matter early in 1995 which was dealt with under the provisions of s 556A of the Crimes Act he had led a crime free life. He lived in "a warm and caring family" and his parents had experienced "no problems with his behaviour prior to the commission of the offence".

He had a good record of employment. At the date of the appeal to this Court, he was in the employment of an employer who was aware of the circumstances of his offence but had found him diligent and trustworthy and had, in substance, expressed particular confidence in his behaviour.

The probation and parole officer, in his report to the sentencing judge, said that it appeared that Mr Lattouf "is not a hard drug user and drinks very little alcohol. He has enjoyed the support of family life and apart from an abrupt end, due to injury, of a promising football career has suffered no adverse circumstances in his life".

A psychiatrist Dr Malcolm Dent, who had examined him, concluded that he suffered a "depressive illness" and the judge said that "... we have a valid psychological explanation of behaviour that is quite out of character". Dr Dent expressed the opinion "that if the prisoner received expert and trained counselling from psychiatrists or psychologists, and in his local area someone such as psychiatrist Dr Osman Ali, then this will assist in relieving his shame and residual depression".

A psychologist Mr KA Barrie had reported that, in his opinion, Mr Lattouf

"is currently suffering levels of anxiety and depression. He is clearly very uncomfortable with his offending behaviour ... He expresses deeply felt remorse for his actions and for the humiliation and distress he had caused his family. He indicated that gaol had taught him to thing (sic) more carefully about the consequences of his actions. George is fortunate in having a family who has remained loyal and supportive and he is grateful for that. He also has a full-time job waiting for him on his release and other positive supports in the community amongst relatives and neighbours. He is speaking in generally positive terms about his future and appears prepared to be guided by his parents and other significant adults in his life."

The judge was satisfied that the robbery was "not a spontaneous agreement as claimed and that some degree of planning went into" it. His Honour concluded that the three accused knew the gun was loaded before they went into the premises and he rejected Mr Pogorilic's claim that he believed the gun was loaded with blanks.

His Honour said:

"Lattouf, who was only nineteen at the time, had led a crime-free life, being a person of good character who comes from a stable and religious family. As I have earlier observed, he has expressed remorse and contrition for his actions and I accept that his participation in the offence is very much unexplained in light of his prior good character and family situation. I am inclined to believe that he was more of a follower than a leader and was caught up in these events without giving it much thought. He has been in custody since 24 April and no doubt the trauma of his short spell in prison has had a dramatic effect upon him.

As has been noted by the Court of Criminal Appeal, the prevalence of this type of offence is a matter of grave social concern. It is now well recognised that the victims of such armed robberies may suffer devastating psychological damage upon the trauma necessarily involved with the commission of such offences. In these circumstances, sentencing judges would be recreant to the trust which the community places in them if they were not to impose sentence consistent with the seriousness and the prevalence of such offence.

Turning now back to Lattouf, having considered both the objective gravity of the offence, together with the strong subjective case that has been made out on behalf of the prisoner, I believe that the appropriate sentence is one of periodic detention."

The principles applicable to the sentencing of a person convicted of an offence such as the present are not in contest. Mr Carmody, for the appellant Director of Public Prosecutions, submitted that a sentence must give effect to the objective circumstances of the offence: R v Rushby (1977) 1 NSWLR 594; R v Dodd (1991) 57 A Crim R 349; the punishment, he submitted, must accord with the general moral sense of the community in relation to the offence in question: R v Geddes [1936] NSWStRp 35; (1936) 36 SR (NSW) 554 at 555. Mr Carmody submitted that this Court has made clear that armed robbery is to be regarded as an offence of the utmost gravity and that it is to be punished by a full time custodial sentence "except in the most exceptional circumstances": reference was made to R v Readman (1990) 47 A Crim R 181; R v Roberts (1994) 73 A Crim R 306. His submission was that in this case the subjective and objective factors to be taken into account in sentencing the respondent Mr Lattouf did not fall and could not be seen to fall within the category of "most exceptional circumstances".

Mr Carmody's submissions suggested, in addition, that the sentence imposed in the present case was inadequate because it would operate to weaken the public perception of the seriousness with which the courts of this State regard the offence of armed robbery and in particular an offence in which a firearm has been used and discharged. His submission has, in effect, suggested that notwithstanding such subjective factors as may in the present case have operated in favour of Mr Lattouf, the effect of a periodic sentence as distinct from a full custodial sentence will inevitably be to weaken the public appreciation of the seriousness with which an offence of this kind is to be regarded.

It is not to be taken from what the Court does in this case that the offence of armed robbery is regarded or will be treated less seriously than has been indicated in cases such as Readman and Roberts. There should be no doubt but that an offence of this kind will, exceptional circumstances apart, attract a full-time custodial sentence. Sentencing judges will not take a different view.

However, in the present case there are, inter alia, several matters which have influenced me to conclude that this appeal should not be upheld. First, this is a case in which it was necessary and appropriate that the judge exercise a careful discretion in arriving at the sentence to be imposed. It is in my opinion necessary that the law allow to a sentencing judge a discretion to determine the sentence appropriate for the particular offence, for the particular offender, and for the circumstances of the particular case. General sentencing principles must be established, so that the community may know the sentences which will be imposed and so that sentencing judges will know the kind and the order of sentence which it is appropriate that they impose. But, of course, principles are necessarily framed in general terms. General principles must, of their nature, be adjusted to the individual case if justice is to be achieved. For this reason, it is in my opinion important in the public interest that the sentencing process recognise and maintain a residual discretion in the sentencing judge.

Second, it is important to recognise the nature of the interests involved in the sentencing process. There is a public interest in the adoption and articulation of sentencing principles which will deter the commission of serious crime and punish those who commit it. That is clear from what I have said as to the principles in Readman and Roberts. But there are other interests to which the sentencing process must have regard; there are other objectives which the sentencing process must seek to achieve. Paramount amongst these is the achievement of justice in the individual case. To see the sentencing process as involving no more than stern punishment for each offender is not merely simplistic; it damages the public interest. A sentencing process which is seen by the public merely as draconian and not just will lose the support of those whom it is designed to protect. If a sentencing process does not achieve justice, it should be be put aside. As I have elsewhere said, if justice is not individual, it is nothing: Kable v Deputy Director of Public Prosecutions (1995) 36 NSWLR 374 at 394.

But, in addition, a sentencing process must be capable of discriminating between cases. There is, as I have said, a public interest in punishment. But if the desire to punish results in a person who would otherwise not become a confirmed criminal becoming such, that sentencing process is inconsistent with the public interest.

It is to be recognised that imprisonment may convert a person who will not be a persistent criminal into one who is. Particularly is this so where the person to be sentenced is a first offender of a comparatively young age whose family circumstances are such that he may, with assistance, not become a criminal. It would be wrong to the individual and costly to the community not to attempt the rehabilitation of such a person.

In my opinion, this is an important factor in the present case. The present offender had no relevant criminal record. He was young, his family circumstances were such as to provide hope for his future in the community. And there were those who were prepared to trust him with employment. That is the kind of case to which heavy punishment is, in my opinion, not appropriate. It is a case in which, notwithstanding the gravity of the offence, it is proper for a sentencing judge, with appropriate safeguards, to seek rehabilitation.

There is in what the judge did an element of faith. The judge placed faith in the assurances given by Mr Lattouf and his family as to his future conduct. If that faith be misplaced, Mr Lattouf will bear the burden of it. He is unlikely to be seen as a person in whose word confidence can be placed and he is apt to be dealt with accordingly. That no doubt he will recognise.

Taking all these matters into account, and bearing in mind the onus which the State bears in an appeal such as the present, I am not satisfied that the sentence in the present case was wrong.

In all the circumstances, I accept the judge's sentence as correct. I would dismiss the appeal.

SULLY J: I have had the benefit of reading in draft the judgment of Mahoney ACJ. With considerable hesitation I concur in the making of an order dismissing this Crown appeal. Because of that hesitation, I wish to make some observations of my own.

I take it to be trite law that the correct first step in the sentencing of any prisoner is the making by the sentencing Judge of a proper assessment of the objective gravity of the particular offence(s). I take it to be equally trite law that the offence of armed robbery is always to be regarded as an offence of the utmost gravity. And, further, I take it to be now well settled, in accordance with the authorities cited by Mahoney ACJ, that such offence requires to be punished by the imposition of a full time custodial sentence "except in the most exceptional circumstances".

Those latter words mean, so far as I am concerned, what they say; and I consider it to be of the first importance that they should be seen by all concerned, not excluding primary sentencing Judges, as meaning what they say. The Courts, - not excluding, if I may say so with respectful bluntness this Court, - do no service to the proper protection of law-abiding citizens by encouraging in any way a perception on the part of a person who is contemplating the carrying out, whether alone or in company, of any form of armed robbery, that he or she can, in effect, proceed upon the assumption that, if caught, a full time custodial sentence can be avoided by a combination of:

[1] A sentencing Judge predisposed to leniency; and

[2} The fact that he or she has never done such a thing previously; and

[3] The fact that he or she has never been previously subject to a full time custodial sentence; and

[4] The fact that he or she can cobble together, should the need arise, a conveniently attractive collection of subjective features.

I acknowledge, of course, that Mahoney ACJ has said in clear terms in his Honour's judgment in the present case that:

"It is not to be taken from what the Court does in this case that the offence of armed robbery is regarded or will be treated less seriously than has been indicated in cases such as Redman and Roberts There should be no doubt but that an offence of this kind will, exceptional circumstances apart, attract a full-time custodial sentence. Sentencing judges will not take a different view."

In the present case, the plain fact is, in my opinion, that the learned sentencing Judge did "take a different view".

I can see readily how the learned sentencing Judge found personal facts that were very favourable to the respondent. I can see as readily that, given those findings, his Honour would have concluded, as does Mahoney ACJ, that, to quote the latter, "(this) is the kind of case to which heavy punishment is, ....... not appropriate".

What I cannot see in the remarks on sentence of the learned primary Judge is a process of reasoning apt to support the proposition that, in accordance with what this Court has said plainly to be the law, there exist in the present case "most exceptional circumstances" such as, alone, will justify less than some full-time custodial sentence for any participant in any armed robbery.

In my opinion it needs to be spelled out clearly by this Court that, absent such exceptional circumstances, it is wrong in principle to suppose that any full-time custodial sentence is "heavy punishment", and apt to erode, in the ways propounded by Mahoney ACJ, public confidence in the law.

I would wish to say plainly that, had I been the proprietor of the shop that was robbed in the present case; or a staff member terrorised at gun-point by one of three armed robbers acting in concert; or the salesman who might easily have been killed or wounded by the shot fired by the gunman accomplice of the respondent; then I do not believe that I would have thought a sentence of periodic detention to have been an appropriate punishment for any of my assailants.

Of course, no Judge can deal with any sentencing problem upon such a basis. That is why our system of criminal justice is administered by independent Judges who have sworn to do justice according to law "without fear or favour, affection or ill-will".

All that said, I think that if the Court is to speak about the need to maintain public confidence in the current system of administering public criminal justice, then, in my opinion, the Court should do what it properly can to reassure all citizens of ordinary good sense and ordinary good will that the Courts and the Judges do understand, and do care about, proper public concerns respecting the punishment and deterrence of serious crime.

My own view, then, is that the learned sentencing Judge did err in not imposing some proper full-time custodial sentence. By "proper", I mean a sentence brining into a measured, sensible balance the objective and subjective factors as established by the evidence before his Honour. In the case of the present respondent, I would have thought that what is often described as a "short, sharp shock", was called for; and that a fixed term of full-time custody in the order of 9 months or thereabouts would have been a justly proportioned outcome.

Initially, I thought that this Court should do in that regard what, in my respectful view, the learned primary Judge should have done. That I have decided, finally, to concur in the dismissal of this appeal, is the result of, and only of, the way in which I think that the element of this Court's over-arching discretion should operate.

The respondent has in fact served 2 months odd of full-time custody. He is entitled to additional consideration on account of the 28 days, spread over 14 week-ends, of periodic detention that he has in fact served pursuant to the sentence imposed in the Court below. Any sentence now to be imposed by this Court, would require, in addition, to be discounted, in accordance with the well established principle governing any re-sentencing by this Court after a successful Crown appeal against sentence. There is only one further matter that I consider to be of real weight for present purposes, and that is the consideration that, to quote again from the judgment of Mahoney ACJ: "(The respondent has) a good record of employment. At the date of appeal to this Court he was in the employment of an employer who was aware of the circumstances of his offence but had found him diligent and trustworthy and had, in substance, expressed particular confidence in his behaviour".

When these various factors are accumulated, then, in my opinion, to impose now an aggregate full-time sentence of the order previously suggested would risk, to a degree that I do not find ultimately acceptable, the disruption, and perhaps the destruction, of the respondent's rehabilitation, which appears to be progressing promisingly. That would risk, in its turn, the mistaking of vindictiveness for vindication.

Even so, the result of this appeal is, so far as I am concerned, a near-run thing. I expressly join Mahoney ACJ in his Honour's explicit warning to this respondent that he should not expect another such chance as he is given by this Court on the present occasion.

ADAMS AJ: I have had the benefit of reading in draft the judgment of Mahoney ACJ and agree with his Honour's conclusion that this appeal should be dismissed and with his Honour's reasons. I wish, however, to emphasise that the crime of armed robbery is extremely serious. Indeed, if I had been the sentencing judge, I would have passed on this appellant a sentence involving full-time custody, despite the strong subjective factors that militated otherwise. Nevertheless, for the reasons set out in the judgment of Mahoney ACJ, I am not prepared to conclude that the sentence passed below was incorrect. Mere difference of opinion does not demonstrate error, let alone appealable error. Having regard both to the nature of the sentencing discretion available and appropriate at first instance and the principles governing, and rightly governing, the exercise by this Court of its appellate jurisdiction when activated by Crown appeals, I agree that the appeal should be dismissed.
